Your portfolio
Total return (portfolio)
—
selected period
i
Time-weighted return: cumulative performance with the effect of deposits and withdrawals stripped out, so it measures how well the underlying investments performed, not how much money you happened to add or remove.
XIRR (overall)
—
annualised, since inception
i
Money-weighted return: the annualised rate that explains your actual cash flows (every buy, sell, and dividend) and today's value. Sensitive to timing — large deposits right before a rally or a crash will move this number even though the investments themselves didn't change.
CAGR
—
i
Compound annual growth rate, derived from the TWR (not the raw NAV ratio) so it reflects investment performance only — the annualised version of total return, ignoring contribution timing.
XIRR vs CAGR
—
XIRR adjusts for cash-flow timing
i
The gap between your money-weighted return and your time-weighted return. A negative gap usually means capital was deployed at less favourable moments than a steady drip-feed would have achieved; a positive gap means the opposite.
Max drawdown
—
i
The largest peak-to-trough decline the portfolio has ever experienced, based on daily NAV. A measure of the worst-case pain endured, independent of how quickly (or slowly) it recovered.
ES3 (reference)
TWR (ES3)
—
same period · div-reinvested
i
ES3's cumulative total return (dividends reinvested) over the same date range as your portfolio's TWR above, so the two figures are directly comparable.
XIRR (ES3)
—
switch-fund · same cash-flow timing
i
A "switch-fund" benchmark: every cash flow into or out of your real portfolio is modelled as buying or selling ES3 units on that same date. Shows what your money-weighted return would have been if you'd invested in ES3 instead, with identical timing.
TWR diff
—
portfolio vs ES3
i
Your portfolio's TWR minus ES3's TWR over the same period. Positive means you beat a passive ES3 investment on a cumulative basis; negative means you fell short.
XIRR diff
—
portfolio vs ES3
i
Your XIRR minus ES3's switch-fund XIRR. Unlike the TWR diff, this comparison accounts for your actual cash-flow timing on both sides, isolating differences in what you invested in rather than when.
Beta
—
daily, selected period
i
How much your portfolio's daily returns move relative to ES3's. 1.00 means it moves in lockstep; below 1.00 means historically lower volatility than the benchmark; above 1.00 means historically higher volatility.
Alpha (annualised)
—
Jensen's alpha, rf=0
i
Jensen's alpha: the annualised excess return your portfolio has produced beyond what its beta (market exposure) alone would predict, assuming a 0% risk-free rate. Positive means skill or selection added value beyond just being more or less exposed to the market; negative means the opposite.
Tracking error
—
annualised stdev of diff
i
How much your daily returns have deviated from ES3's, expressed as an annualised standard deviation. A high tracking error means your portfolio behaves very differently from the benchmark day to day, for better or worse.
Information ratio
—
excess return ÷ tracking error
i
Annualised excess return over ES3, divided by tracking error. Measures whether your outperformance (or underperformance) has been a consistent, repeatable pattern or just a few volatile swings.
Risk & ratios
Sharpe ratio
—
i
Return earned per unit of total risk taken (both upside and downside volatility count equally). Above 1.0 is generally considered good; below 1.0 suggests the return hasn't fully compensated for the volatility involved.
Sortino ratio
—
i
Like the Sharpe ratio, but only penalises downside volatility — upside swings don't count against it. A Sortino ratio much higher than Sharpe suggests your volatility has skewed positive (more big up-days than big down-days).
Calmar ratio
—
CAGR ÷ max drawdown
i
Annualised return (CAGR) divided by the single worst drawdown ever experienced. Unlike Sharpe and Sortino, which look at typical day-to-day behaviour, Calmar focuses specifically on recovery from the worst-case loss — a low ratio means a long road back from the deepest drop.
Win rate (daily)
—
i
The percentage of days the portfolio's value increased. Below 50% is normal for long-only equity portfolios and isn't inherently a bad sign — the size of wins versus losses (captured by Sharpe/Sortino) usually matters more than how often each occurs.
